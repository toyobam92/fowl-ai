This week goes deeper on the practical side: how the hidden economy of AI evaluation actually works, and where the next wave of roles is forming.
Issue 1 ended with a claim: domain experts who can tell a model it's wrong have something genuinely scarce. Several of you replied asking the same question — how do I actually get paid for that?

The answer involves understanding infrastructure most people never see. AI evaluation platforms run on quality scores. Those scores determine which tasks you access, what you earn, and whether you stay active on the platform at all. This week is about that infrastructure — and five other signals worth your attention.
This Week — 5 Developments
01
The quality score is a hidden credit score for AI work — and most people don't know it exists
Every major AI evaluation platform — Outlier, Mercor, DataAnnotation, Mindrift — runs an internal quality scoring system. Your score is calculated from accuracy, consistency, and reasoning quality on completed tasks. It determines which task queues you're eligible for. High scorers access domain-expert queues paying $50–200+/hr. Low scorers stay in the generic annotation queue at $15–20/hr — or get quietly deprioritised. The score is rarely visible to the worker. Most people who "applied and never heard back" have a low score, not a rejection.
Why it matters · The platform is always evaluating you. Even onboarding tasks are scored. The professionals earning serious money treat every task as an audition.
Who benefits · Anyone who approaches the work with the same discipline they'd bring to a client deliverable. Speed and volume are not the signal. Accuracy and reasoning quality are.
The signal · Your quality score is more important than your CV on these platforms. A verifiable track record of high-accuracy domain evaluation is a professional asset — and it compounds.
From the inside — The workers earning $100+/hr on Mercor are not the ones with the most impressive LinkedIn profiles. They're the ones who understood, early, that every task was a test — and treated it as one.
02
AI-powered hiring is creating an invisible ceiling for mid-career professionals
Data from LinkedIn and Indeed shows that AI-assisted resume screening is systematically filtering out professionals with 8–15 years of experience. The models are optimised on recent hire data — which skews toward candidates who explicitly list AI tool keywords. Domain expertise built over a decade doesn't register. Senior professionals are being screened out before a human sees their application, then wondering why response rates have collapsed.
Why it matters · The ATS keyword problem isn't new. But AI-assisted screening has amplified it and extended it into interview scheduling and take-home assessment ranking. The filter is earlier and harder to see.
Who benefits · Professionals who update their positioning — not just their skills. "10 years in financial risk management" needs to become "financial risk management with AI workflow integration" before the screen, not after.
The signal · The resume is now a document written for a model, not a human. The model reads for keywords and recency. Optimise accordingly — without sacrificing the depth that makes you valuable once you're in the room.
From the inside — The irony: the professionals getting filtered out by AI hiring tools are often the most qualified to evaluate AI outputs. The fix isn't to pretend the tools don't exist — it's to speak their language in the document, and your own language in the conversation.
03
EU AI Act: 8 weeks to August 2 — what "AI literacy" actually requires employers to do
Article 4 of the EU AI Act requires organisations to ensure all staff who interact with AI systems have "a sufficient level of AI literacy." The law doesn't define sufficient. HR and legal teams across regulated industries are now writing that definition themselves — in audit-ready language. Early movers are building assessments that test calibrated judgment (can you tell when an AI output is wrong?) rather than tool familiarity. That distinction matters for what gets hired for and what gets trained for.
Why it matters · "AI literacy" is becoming a compliance checkbox — but the organisations doing it seriously are defining it as critical evaluation capacity, not prompt familiarity. That's a hiring signal worth watching.
The signal · The first wave of "AI Literacy Lead" roles is appearing on job boards. These are the people defining what the standard means inside their organisation. Not a technical role. A judgment role.
From the inside — The companies scrambling right now are the ones who spent 2025 deploying AI tools and skipped the governance layer. The ones ahead built Human-in-the-Loop checkpoints early. The audit question isn't "do your staff use AI?" — it's "can they tell when it's wrong, and do you have a record of them doing so?"
04
Microsoft's Copilot data reveals a productivity split — and it's widening
Microsoft's 2026 Work Trend Index: 70% of Copilot users report time savings. But only 32% report using that saved time for higher-value work. The majority are using AI to do the same tasks faster. The professionals pulling ahead are using AI to take on work they previously couldn't — expanding scope, not just compressing time. The productivity gap between these two groups is measurable and growing.
Why it matters · Speed is table stakes. The leverage comes from what you do with the time AI creates. Most people are optimising the wrong variable.
Who benefits · Professionals who are deliberately redirecting AI-freed time toward high-judgment, high-visibility work — not just clearing their inbox faster.
The signal · "I use Copilot to save time" is a feature, not a differentiator. "I use Copilot to take on engagements I previously couldn't" is a career strategy.
From the inside — The 32% are not necessarily more skilled. They're more intentional. They decided in advance what the freed time was for. That's a decision, not a circumstance.
05
Agent escalation is becoming a job category — and it doesn't have a name yet
Following ServiceNow's autonomous workforce launch, enterprise job boards are showing a new posting pattern: roles built around managing the edge cases that AI agents escalate to humans. These are not orchestration roles (Issue 1). They're human-in-the-loop escalation specialists — people who handle the ambiguous, high-stakes 10% that agents can't resolve. The job: receive an escalation, make a decision with limited context, document the reasoning, send it back. It's a new workflow, and organisations are building headcount around it.
Why it matters · This is what "staying on the human side of the automation line" looks like in practice. The role doesn't require building AI — it requires being reliably right when the AI is wrong.
The signal · Watch for titles like "AI Exception Handler," "Escalation Review Specialist," and "Human Review Lead" multiplying through Q3 and Q4. The ServiceNow launch is a starting gun, not a peak.
From the inside — The skills that matter here are domain depth and decision quality under uncertainty — not technical. The professionals who've spent years making judgment calls in high-stakes environments are structurally well-positioned for this. They just need to see it as a category.

"I applied to Outlier three months ago and never heard back." Almost always a quality score issue from onboarding tasks, not a capacity hold. Reapply after 90 days and treat the first 10 tasks as a formal assessment — slow down, cite your reasoning.
"How do I get into the expert queue?" Consistency beats speed. Platforms track variance — workers who score 95% consistently outperform workers who score 100% on half and 70% on half. Steady and accurate beats brilliant and erratic.
The parallel platform strategy is real. Community consensus: run DataAnnotation as your baseline (reliable payout, steady volume), qualify for Mercor as the upside (slower to onboard, higher ceiling). Don't wait for one before starting the other.
"Is the Mercor technical screen actually hard?" Yes, but not in the way people expect. It's not testing tool knowledge — it's testing whether you can identify where an AI output went wrong and articulate the failure mode clearly. Domain knowledge is the differentiator.
From the inside — The community has figured out what the platforms haven't made explicit: quality score mechanics, optimal task pacing, parallel platform strategy. The people sharing this information are the ones earning the most. Pay attention to them.
🪪 Emerging Career Title
This week
AI Literacy Lead
The EU AI Act's Article 4 enforcement deadline is creating a new internal role: the person responsible for defining what "sufficient AI literacy" means inside their organisation, building the assessment framework, and ensuring the standard holds up under audit. This is not an engineering role. It's a governance and judgment role sitting at the intersection of HR, legal, and operations.

The job: translate a vague legal standard into a testable, auditable competency framework. Then build the training and assessment infrastructure around it. Organisations that do this well will have a defensible compliance position and a workforce that can actually evaluate AI outputs. The ones who do it poorly will have a checkbox.
How to position: Relevant background includes learning & development, compliance, AI evaluation experience, or domain expertise in a regulated industry. The differentiator is being able to write the competency standard itself — specific, testable, defensible. Pair with EU AI Act Article 4 knowledge and NIST AI RMF literacy. The role doesn't require building AI — it requires understanding it well enough to assess others on it.
