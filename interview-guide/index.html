Introduction

What AI platform interviews are actually testing

The Mercor interview is a technical background interview — not a test of how well you understand AI evaluation theory. They are not asking how you would evaluate AI outputs. They are asking: who are you, what have you built, and can you think through a technical problem clearly?

Treat it like a senior technical interview at a company, not like an AI evaluation screening. The AI interviewer is verifying that your background is real, your projects are genuine, and your technical knowledge holds up under specific questions.

What actually happens

You will be asked about your background, your projects, and your technical experience. For technical roles, they may show you a piece of code and ask you to identify what is wrong. Come prepared with specific project stories and your technical stack — not with a prepared speech about AI evaluation philosophy.

The three things Mercor's interview actually tests

  • Technical depth — Do you know your domain well enough to answer specific questions about it? Vague answers fail. Named tools, real numbers, and specific outcomes pass.
  • Project credibility — Are your projects real, detailed, and measurable? They will ask follow-up questions. If you cannot go deeper, the project was not real enough to mention.
  • Problem-solving under pressure — Can you look at a technical problem — a piece of code, a broken query, a flawed approach — and explain clearly what is wrong and why? This is the practical part of the interview.

Chapter 01

The Mercor AI video screening: a complete walkthrough

Mercor uses an AI interviewer — a video call with no human on the other end. This makes most people nervous. It should not. The AI is consistent, does not judge nervousness, and rewards clear, specific answers. Interviews run approximately 20 minutes. You have up to 3 official retakes across all applications if needed.

Critical — read before you start

Use Edge or Safari only. Firefox fails Mercor's AI interview system. Mobile browsers are also incompatible. Opening the interview in the wrong browser is the most preventable reason people fail before they even speak. Use a laptop or desktop, Edge or Safari, with a stable internet connection. Headphones with an external microphone are strongly recommended.

Before you start: the 15-minute prep ritual

Write out these four things before you open the Mercor link:

  1. Your domain in one sentence — "I am a data scientist with 5 years in financial services predictive modeling." Not "I work in data." Specific industry, specific function, specific years. This is the first thing you will say and it sets the tone for the whole interview.
  2. Three projects using STAR format — For each project: Situation (1–2 sentences), Action (what you built/did and which tools — 2–3 sentences), Result (specific measurable outcome). Write these out fully before the interview. The AI will ask follow-up questions — if you cannot go deeper on a project, do not mention it.
  3. Your technical stack, named specifically — List every tool, language, framework, and platform you actually use. "Python, SQL, XGBoost, scikit-learn, Spark, Airflow" is a complete answer. "Python and some ML tools" is not. The AI scores named technologies.
  4. Code review warm-up — For technical roles, Mercor may show you a piece of code in the interview and ask what is wrong. Before your interview, spend 20–30 minutes reviewing code in your domain — find a bug, explain it clearly out loud. Practice saying: "The issue here is X because Y, which would cause Z in production." Explanation structure matters as much as spotting the bug.
  5. Record and review two practice answers — Record yourself answering: (1) your background, and (2) your most challenging project. Watch them back. If anything sounds vague or takes more than 90 seconds, rewrite it. You can also paste your resume into ChatGPT and ask for Mercor-style technical interview questions to practice with.

The most common questions — and exactly how to answer them

Tell me about your background and experience.
Lead with domain, then tools, then impact. "I am a data scientist specializing in predictive modeling for financial services. I work primarily in Python and SQL, building models that drive customer retention and credit decisioning. Most recently I built an uplift model that improved campaign ROI by 23%." That is the template.
Do not start with where you went to school. Start with what you do professionally and what it produces.
What kind of AI-related work have you done?
Be specific about models, tools, and judgment. "I have built and deployed ML models using scikit-learn and XGBoost. I regularly evaluate model output for bias and accuracy before production. I also review AI-generated code as part of my current team's workflow." If you have not used AI in your job directly, pivot to judgment: "I evaluate analytical outputs for correctness, which maps directly to AI evaluation work."
How would you rate your expertise in [your domain] on a scale of 1–10?
Never say 10. Say 7–8 and explain. "I would say 7 or 8. I have strong depth in [specific area] but I am always learning, particularly in [adjacent area]. In [specific area] I am confident I can catch errors a generalist would miss." This signals honest calibration — exactly what AI platforms want.
Platforms specifically want people who know the boundaries of their expertise. Overconfidence is a red flag.
Walk me through your most technically challenging project.
Use STAR: Situation (context + the problem, 1–2 sentences), Action (what you built, which tools, and any non-obvious decisions — 3–4 sentences), Result (measurable outcome, 1–2 sentences). Example: "I was tasked with improving credit card retention. I built an uplift model using Python and XGBoost to identify persuadable customers — the non-obvious part was correctly handling the counterfactual, since most models were predicting churn rather than persuadability. The result was a 23% improvement in campaign ROI across 4 million cardholders." That is a complete answer in under 60 seconds. Have 2–3 of these ready. The AI will pick one and ask follow-ups.
The follow-up questions are where most people get caught. If you cannot explain the technical decisions you made in detail, do not lead with that project.
Technical questions about your domain
Mercor asks domain-specific technical questions based on what you put in your profile. For data science roles: questions about model selection, statistical reasoning, feature engineering, or SQL. For engineering roles: system design, debugging approaches, architecture tradeoffs. These are not trick questions — they are calibration questions. Answer directly and specifically. If you are not sure, say what you do know and acknowledge the limit. A specific partial answer is better than a vague complete one.
The questions will match your stated expertise. If you say you specialize in NLP, expect NLP questions. Do not claim expertise areas you cannot defend under follow-up.

More Mercor questions — and the technical task

Tell me about your background and experience.
This is how most Mercor interviews open. Lead with domain, industry, and years — then tools, then one specific project outcome. "I am a data scientist with 10 years in fintech and payments. I work in Python and SQL, building predictive models for customer retention and fraud risk. Most recently I have been processing billions of transactions quarterly at Visa." Do not start with your degree. Start with what you do and what it produces.
The AI uses your answer to calibrate the rest of the interview. A specific opening gets you specific follow-ups in your domain. A vague opening gets you generic questions.
Live code review — "Here is some code. What issues do you see?"
Mercor may show you a code snippet during the interview and ask you to find the problem. This is not a trick — they want to see how you think out loud, not just whether you spot the bug. Walk through it step by step: "I am reading through this and I can see it is trying to [purpose]. On line X, there is [issue] — this would cause [specific problem] because [reason]. I would fix it by [approach]." Structure matters. Saying "there is a bug on line 12" without explanation is a weak answer. Explaining the bug, why it fails, and how to fix it is a strong one.
If you genuinely cannot spot the issue, say what you do see and where you would look next. "I am not seeing an obvious bug, but I would check [X] because [reason]" is better than silence or a guess.

After the video interview — the take-home technical task

After passing the video screening, Mercor typically sends a domain-matched technical task. For data and ML roles this usually involves:

  • Reviewing a piece of Python or SQL code and identifying specific bugs or inefficiencies
  • Answering a domain-specific technical question (statistics, modeling, data interpretation)
  • Explaining a technical concept clearly and accurately
How to approach the take-home task

Take your time — there is no speed bonus. Show your reasoning, not just your answer. "This query will fail because it uses a correlated subquery inside a GROUP BY without aggregation — specifically on line 4, the subquery is recalculated for every row which causes [issue]. The fix is to move it to a CTE." That level of explanation is what passes. "The query has a bug" does not.

After you submit

Response time from Mercor varies from 24 hours to 2 weeks. If you hear nothing after 10 days, reapply with a slightly more specific profile. Many successful candidates applied 2–3 times before being matched to a project. You officially have up to 3 retakes.

What happens the first week after you pass

Passing the Mercor screening does not mean projects immediately appear. Here is what the first week actually looks like:

  • Day 1–2: You receive a confirmation email. Your profile is now visible to Mercor's matching system. No projects yet — you are in the queue.
  • Day 3–5: If your profile matches an active project, you will receive an invitation to review project details and accept. Response speed matters — accept or decline within 24 hours or it moves to the next candidate.
  • Day 5–10: First tasks appear. Treat the first 5 tasks as your extended audition. Your quality score on these tasks determines whether you are matched to higher-value projects or lower-value ones. Do not rush them.
  • If no project in 2 weeks: This usually means your stated domain is not currently matched to active projects — not that you failed. Update your profile to add adjacent skills or domains, and check back. New projects are onboarded regularly.

Outlier AI & DataAnnotation: passing the skills assessment

How Outlier's assessment works

Outlier does not do a video interview. Instead they run you through a series of tasks to calibrate your ability. These typically include evaluating AI-generated writing, rating code quality, or answering domain questions. Your scores on these tasks determine which task categories you are offered — and what those tasks pay.

Critical mistake to avoid

Do not rush through the onboarding assessment to start earning faster. Your score on the first 10–15 tasks is the single most important factor in determining your earning ceiling on Outlier. A bad start is very hard to recover from. Slow down.

What Outlier's scoring system rewards

BehaviorScore Impact
Detailed, specific written feedbackHigh positive
Correct ratings with reasoningHigh positive
Fast but accurate completionsModerate positive
Ratings without explanationNeutral
Rushed or inconsistent ratingsNegative
High disagreement with consensusNegative

DataAnnotation: the coding task

DataAnnotation's entry process usually involves completing a sample coding or writing task. For the coding task:

  • Write clean, commented code — not just working code
  • If asked to evaluate AI code, explain what is wrong and why, not just mark it incorrect
  • Show your thinking in any explanatory fields provided
Tip

On DataAnnotation, explicitly mention Python, SQL, or JavaScript in your profile if those are your languages. Platform task matching is heavily keyword-driven — the right words on your profile unlock the right (better-paying) task categories.

Handshake AI: the accessible Tier 2 entry point

Handshake AI's Fellowship program is one of the most accessible routes into paid AI evaluation work — and one of the most underestimated. It has disbursed over $100M to 100,000+ fellows and pays up to $100/hr for the right profiles. Important: available to US residents with valid work authorization only.

Why it's worth applying early

Handshake AI's onboarding is significantly less intimidating than Mercor's AI video interview. It is a strong parallel application — apply to both at the same time. Handshake AI income can start while you are still waiting on Mercor approval.

How Handshake AI's screening works

Handshake AI separates applicants into two tracks: domain experts (deep academic or professional expertise in a specific field) and motivated generalists (strong communication, analytical ability). Your profile and background determine which track you enter — and expert-track work pays significantly more.

  1. Profile completion — Fill every section of your profile with specific domain language. Do not leave fields vague. The platform matches based on keyword signals in your profile, not resume parsing.
  2. Skills assessment — Depending on your domain, you will receive evaluation tasks to calibrate your ability. Treat these like the Outlier onboarding: accuracy over speed, detailed written reasoning over fast clicks.
  3. Project matching — Once qualified, Handshake AI matches you to fellowship opportunities. Response time and availability matter — projects are assigned based on who responds first.
How to position yourself for the expert track
State your domain explicitly in every profile field. "Data professional" puts you in the generalist track. "Data scientist with 5 years in fintech predictive modeling — Python, SQL, XGBoost" puts you in the expert track. The difference in pay rate between the two tracks is significant. Always position for expert even if you are not certain you qualify — let the assessment decide.
List your specific industry (fintech, healthcare, legal, energy) prominently. Handshake AI project matching is domain-specific — the right industry tag unlocks the right projects.
Alongside Mercor, not instead of it

Handshake AI and Mercor target different task types and pay at different rates. Apply to both simultaneously. Use Handshake AI income as a stable baseline while Mercor projects are intermittent or between engagements.

The language that scores high — and the language that doesn't

Across all AI platform interviews, there is a consistent pattern in what scores well and what does not. Here are the most important before/after comparisons.

Weak answer

"I have a lot of experience in data science and I'm familiar with machine learning."

Strong answer

"I have 5 years in financial services data science, primarily building propensity and uplift models in Python. I work with scikit-learn, XGBoost, and SQL daily."

Weak answer

"This answer from the AI seems wrong to me."

Strong answer

"The AI's statistical conclusion is incorrect because it conflates correlation with causation in step 3. The correct approach would be to control for X variable before drawing that inference."

Weak profile

"Data professional with experience in analytics and AI tools."

Strong profile

"Data scientist with 5 years in fintech. Python, SQL, scikit-learn, XGBoost. Predictive modeling, A/B testing, uplift modeling for credit and retention products."

Weak answer

"I would rate my expertise as 9 out of 10."

Strong answer

"I would say 7 or 8. I have deep expertise in predictive modeling and financial data, but I am still developing my knowledge of large language model evaluation specifically."

The rule

Every answer should answer two implicit questions: what domain? and how specifically? If your answer does not answer both, rewrite it before you say it.

The 7-day interview prep plan

  • Day 1
    Write your expertise statement

    One paragraph: your domain, your tools, your years of experience, and one specific project outcome. This becomes the foundation of every answer you give.

  • Day 2
    Prepare 3 project stories

    For each project: problem → approach → tools used → measurable result. Write these out in full. Practice saying them out loud until they sound natural, not memorized.

  • Day 3
    Practice the core Q&A out loud

    Record yourself answering: background, expertise rating, AI work experience, error identification example, why AI evaluation. Watch it back. Fix anything vague.

  • Day 4
    Update all platform profiles

    Use your expertise statement language. Add specific tools and technologies. Remove vague phrases. Make sure your domain and industry are stated clearly on every profile.

  • Day 5
    Apply to Mercor

    Set aside 45 uninterrupted minutes. Good lighting, quiet background, no phone. Treat it like a real interview even though the other side is an AI.

  • Day 6
    Complete Outlier and DataAnnotation onboarding

    Take the skills assessment slowly. Write detailed feedback. Do not rush. Your early scores determine your earning ceiling.

  • Day 7
    Apply to Invisible AI + review and adjust

    Submit your Invisible AI application with your strongest domain framing. Review how the week went and refine your profile language based on any feedback received.

Final Word

What separates people who pass from people who don't

After going through this process across multiple platforms, the pattern is consistent. The people who pass AI platform screenings are not necessarily the most technically skilled. They are the most precisely self-aware.

They know exactly what they are good at, they can say it clearly, and they can demonstrate it in their answers and their feedback quality. That is the skill these platforms are buying.

The single most important thing

Before any interview or assessment, write your expertise statement. One paragraph: domain, tools, years, one real outcome. If you cannot write it clearly, you cannot say it clearly. The writing comes first.

If you get rejected

Rejection from Mercor is not permanent. Mercor officially allows up to 3 retakes across all applications. Wait 2–3 weeks, refine your profile language to be more specific, and reapply. Many people who now earn $95/hr on Mercor were rejected on their first attempt. The difference was specificity and framing, not skill.

After you pass

Once you are active on a platform, your rating and consistency determine everything. Deliver careful, well-explained work consistently. Ask for higher-tier tasks as your score builds. Treat the first 30 days as your audition for the rates you actually want.
